# 19th century Interval Rules — Source Notes

**Original note file:** `19c_interval_rules_notes.md`

## Scope
- Decade rows are intentionally repeated. The consulted 19th-century harmony/counterpoint sources state rules by practice/species/chord type, not by decade. The YEAR column is a decade bucket, not a claim that the rule changed in that decade.
- Values marked "no source max" mean the sources allow or discuss the category but do not give a universal numeric maximum.
- "Structural" means a real harmonic/counterpoint interval between voices, not a passing, neighboring, ornamental, or suspension event.
- Harmony rows assume common-practice four-part pedagogy. Counterpoint rows assume strict/species counterpoint, with freer chromatic usage only where explicitly marked.

## Source-bounded rule base
1. Ebenezer Prout, Harmony: Its Theory and Practice, Ch. I: intervals, compound intervals, consonance/dissonance, and note that 9ths/11ths/13ths are special in harmony. https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Harmony%3A_Its_Theory_and_Practice/Chapter_1
2. Prout, Ch. IV: prohibition/limits on consecutive unisons, octaves, fifths, fourths, seconds, sevenths, and ninths; static repetition is not treated as consecutive motion. https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Harmony%3A_Its_Theory_and_Practice/Chapter_4
3. Prout, Ch. IX: dominant seventh resolution: the leading-tone third rises and the seventh falls by step; fifth handling avoids consecutive fifths. https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Harmony%3A_Its_Theory_and_Practice/Chapter_9
4. Prout, contents for Chs. XVI-XIX: thirteenth chords, augmented-sixth chords, diatonic discords, and suspensions; especially the distinction between older preparation rules and modern freer treatment of essential discords. https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Page%3AHarmony_-_its_theory_and_practice.djvu/13
5. Grove / Wikisource, A Dictionary of Music and Musicians, "Strict Counterpoint": species counterpoint treatment of concords, passing discords, suspensions, and resolution. https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/A_Dictionary_of_Music_and_Musicians/Strict_Counterpoint

## Abbreviation decisions
- b3 / m3 = minor third.
- b6 = minor sixth.
- bb7 = diminished seventh.
- b7 = minor seventh.
- b9 = minor ninth.
- #4 / b5 = tritone spelling split: #4 treated as augmented fourth, b5 as diminished fifth.
- #11 = compound augmented fourth.
- b13 / 13 = compound sixth in counterpoint, but 13th-chord dissonance in harmony.

## Caution
#9 and #11 are not handled in the consulted 19th-century sources as modern jazz-style chord extensions. They are therefore marked as rare/chromatic/contextual rather than assigned invented standard resolutions.
